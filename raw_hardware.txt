1. FDM (Fused Deposition Modeling)
🚀 Meet the Most Popular 3D Printing Technology
Imagine you have a tube of toothpaste. When you squeeze it, toothpaste comes out from the nozzle and creates a line.
Now imagine replacing toothpaste with melted plastic. That's exactly how an FDM printer works!
FDM stands for: Fused Deposition Modeling
It is the most widely used 3D printing technology in: Schools, Colleges, Industries, Research Labs, Homes
🤔 Have You Seen a Cake Being Decorated?
A baker uses a piping bag. Cream comes out from the nozzle. Layer by layer decorations are created. An FDM printer
works in a very similar way. Instead of cream, it uses plastic filament. Instead of a piping bag, it uses an
extruder and nozzle. Instead of a cake, it creates a 3D object.
🎬 What Happens When You Click Print?
Let's follow the journey of a filament.
Step 1: Meet Mr. Filament 🧵
Everything starts with a filament spool. Think of filament as the raw material of FDM printing. Common materials
include PLA, PETG, ABS, and TPU. The filament waits patiently on the spool.
Fun Fact
A standard filament spool contains around 330 meters of filament. That's longer than 3 football fields!
Step 2: The Extruder Pulls the Filament
Think of the extruder as:
The Printer's Feeding System
Its job is simple: "Pull filament from the spool and send it to the hotend." Without the extruder, there is no
filament, no print, and no fun.
Interactive Question
❓ What happens if the extruder stops feeding filament?
✅ Printing stops immediately.
Step 3: Welcome to the Hotend 🔥
Now the filament reaches the hottest place in the printer. The hotend is like a Tiny Plastic Melting Factory. Inside
the hotend, Solid Plastic becomes Soft Plastic and then Molten Plastic.
Typical Temperatures
PLA → 200°C
PETG → 240°C
ABS → 250°C
That's hotter than boiling water!
Did You Know?
The nozzle can become hot enough to cause burns within seconds. Never touch it during printing.
Step 4: The Nozzle Starts Drawing
The nozzle acts like a pen, but instead of ink, it deposits molten plastic. The nozzle follows instructions from the
printer and starts drawing the first layer.
Imagine This
Drawing a square, then another square on top, then another, and then another, hundreds of times. Eventually, a 3D
object appears.
Step 5: The Build Plate Becomes a Construction Site
The build plate is where the magic begins. Think of it as the Foundation of a Building. Every object starts from the
first layer. If the first layer fails, the entire print fails.
Real-Life Example
If the foundation of a house is weak, the house may collapse. The same thing happens in 3D printing.

🤖 Meet the Brain of the Printer
Every printer has a brain. This brain is called the Controller Board. It receives instructions from the computer and
then tells the motors where to move, the hotend how much to heat, and the extruder how much filament to push.
Think of It Like This
Human Brain → Muscles → Movement
Similarly,
Controller Board → Motors → Printing.

2. SLA (Stereolithography)
🚀 Meet the Technology That Prints with Liquid
Imagine you have a bucket filled with liquid. Now imagine a magical flashlight that can instantly turn that liquid
into solid plastic wherever the light touches. Sounds like science fiction? That's exactly how SLA 3D Printing
works. Unlike FDM printers that melt filament, SLA printers use Liquid Photopolymer Resin and UV Light to create
objects layer by layer.
🤔 What Makes SLA Special?
Have you ever looked at a dental model or a jewelry prototype and wondered how it has such smooth surfaces and tiny
details?
That's where SLA shines.
SLA printers can produce:
✅ Smooth Surfaces
✅ High Accuracy
✅ Tiny Details
✅ Professional Quality Models
🎬 What Happens When You Click Print?
Let's follow a drop of resin through its journey.
Step 1: Meet Mr. Resin 💧
Everything starts with a liquid called Photopolymer Resin. This special liquid reacts when exposed to UV light. When
UV light touches the resin, Liquid becomes Solid.
Magic? No. Science? Yes.
Fun Fact
A bottle of resin may look like ordinary liquid, but under UV light, it transforms into solid plastic within
seconds.
Step 2: The Resin Tank Holds the Material
The resin is stored inside a container called the Resin Vat. Think of it as a swimming pool filled with resin. The
printer continuously uses resin from this tank during printing.
Responsibilities
✅ Stores resin
✅ Provides material for printing
✅ Maintains resin level
Real-Life Analogy
FDM Printer → Uses Filament Spool
SLA Printer → Uses Resin Tank.
Step 3: The Magic Window (FEP Film)
At the bottom of the resin tank is a transparent sheet called the FEP Film. Think of it as a glass window. The UV
light must pass through this film before reaching the resin.
Why is FEP Important?
Without the FEP film:
❌ Resin leaks
❌ Light cannot pass correctly
❌ Prints fail
Easy Memory Trick
FEP Film = Printer's Window
Step 4: The UV Light Engine Wakes Up 🔵
Now the real magic begins. The printer shines UV light through the FEP film. Wherever the light touches, Liquid
Resin becomes a Solid Layer.
Think About It
Imagine drawing shapes on water. Every time the UV light shines, a solid layer appears.
Interactive Question
❓ What happens if UV light does not reach the resin?
✅ The resin remains liquid.
Step 5: The Build Platform Pulls the Print Up
This is the coolest part. Unlike FDM printing where the object grows upward from the bed, in SLA printing, the
object hangs upside down.
What Happens?
A layer cures → Build platform moves upward → Fresh resin flows underneath → Next layer cures → Process repeats.
Eventually hundreds or thousands of layers combine to form the complete model.
🏗️ How an SLA Printer Builds Objects
Resin Tank → UV Light Source → FEP Film → Build Platform → Printed Part.

🦺 Safety Rules for SLA Printing
Unlike FDM printing, SLA printing involves chemicals. Safety is extremely important.

Wear Safety Glasses 🥽 → Protect your eyes during resin handling.

Wash Hands After Handling Resin → Even when gloves are used.

3. SLS (Selective Laser Sintering)
🚀 Imagine Printing Without Filament or Resin
By now, you've seen: 🧵 FDM uses Filament. 💧 SLA uses Liquid Resin. But what if we could print using powder? That's
exactly what SLS does. SLS stands for: Selective Laser Sintering. Instead of melting filament or curing resin, SLS
uses a powerful laser to fuse tiny powder particles together layer by layer.
🤔 What Makes SLS Special?
Imagine building a sandcastle. Normally, you need support structures to hold things together. In SLS: the
surrounding powder automatically supports the printed part.
This means:
✅ No Support Structures
✅ Complex Geometries
✅ Functional Parts
✅ Industrial Applications
Fun Fact
An SLS printer can print dozens of different parts in a single job because all parts are supported by the
surrounding powder.
🎬 What Happens When You Click Print?
Let's follow a tiny powder particle through its journey.
Step 1: Meet Mr. Powder ⚪
Everything begins with a fine powder. Common materials include:
Nylon (PA12)
PA11
Glass Filled Nylon
Engineering Polymers
The powder feels similar to Fine flour or talcum powder.
Step 2: The Powder Feed Chamber Supplies Material
Think of the powder feed chamber as: The Printer's Storage Tank. Its job is to continuously provide fresh powder.
Responsibilities
✅ Stores powder
✅ Supplies material
✅ Maintains powder levels
Real-Life Analogy
FDM Printer → Filament Spool
SLS Printer → Powder Feed Chamber
Step 3: The Recoater Spreads a Powder Layer
Now a component called the: Recoater Blade moves across the build area. Its job: Spread an extremely thin layer of
powder.
Think of It Like This. Imagine using a ruler to spread sand evenly across a surface. That's exactly what the
recoater does.
Typical Layer Thickness: 50–150 Microns. That's thinner than a human hair.
Step 4: The Laser Starts Drawing 🔥
Now the exciting part begins. A high-power laser scans the powder bed.
Wherever the laser touches: Powder Particles → Fuse Together → Solid Layer Forms.
Important Note
The laser does NOT melt the entire bed. It only fuses the areas that belong to the part. Everything else remains
powder.
Interactive Question
❓ What happens to the powder that is not touched by the laser?
✅ It remains loose and acts as natural support material.
Step 5: Build Platform Moves Down
After one layer is completed: Build Platform moves down. A new layer of powder is spread. The laser scans again.
Another layer forms.
This Process Repeats
Spread Powder → Laser Scan → Platform Moves → Repeat → Until the complete object is built.
🎯 The Amazing Part
At the end of printing: The entire build chamber looks like a box filled with powder. Inside that powder: Hidden
parts are waiting. Students often describe this as: "Archaeology for Engineers" because you dig the parts out of the
powder.
🏗️ SLS Construction Flow
Powder Feed Chamber → Recoater Blade → Powder Bed → Laser System → Finished Part

4. DED (Directed Energy Deposition)
🚀 Imagine Repairing a Broken Metal Part Instead of Replacing It. Suppose an aircraft engine blade is damaged.
Traditional options: 1. Replace the entire component. 2. Expensive repair process. 3. Long downtime. Now imagine a
machine that can: Add metal exactly where required, Repair worn surfaces, Build large metal parts, Add features to
existing components. This is exactly what DED technology does.
🤔 What is DED?
DED stands for: Directed Energy Deposition. It is a metal additive manufacturing process where: Metal Material +
High Energy Source combine to create metal parts layer by layer. Unlike powder bed technologies such as SLS or DMLS,
DED deposits material directly where it is needed.
Think About It Like Welding. If welding joins two metal parts together, DED is like: Extremely Precise Automated
Welding Controlled by a Computer. The machine continuously feeds metal while simultaneously melting it.
Why is DED Special?
Most 3D printing technologies start with an empty build plate.
DED can:
✅ Build New Parts
✅ Repair Existing Parts
✅ Add Features to Components
✅ Manufacture Large Metal Structures
Real-World Example
An aerospace company repairs a damaged turbine blade. Instead of replacing the entire blade: DED adds metal only to
the damaged area.
This saves:
💰 Cost
⏳ Time
🔩 Material
🎬 What Happens When You Click Print?
Let's follow a metal particle through its journey.
Step 1: Meet Mr. Metal 🔩
DED uses metal as raw material. This can be: Metal Wire or Metal Powder.
Common Materials: 🔩 Stainless Steel, ✈️ Titanium, 🚗 Aluminum, 🔥 Inconel, 🛡️ Cobalt Chrome.
Fun Fact: Some DED machines use the same titanium alloys used in aircraft and spacecraft.
Step 2: Material Feeds Through the Nozzle
The material feed system continuously delivers metal toward the deposition zone. Think of it like: Feeding Wire into
a Welding Torch.
Responsibilities
✅ Supplies material
✅ Maintains flow rate
✅ Ensures consistency
Step 3: The Energy Source Wakes Up ⚡
Now the machine activates a powerful energy source. Possible energy sources include: Laser, Plasma Arc, Electron
Beam.
What Does It Do? The energy source generates enough heat to melt metal instantly.
Think of It Like: A concentrated energy beam acting as a metal melting tool.
Step 4: The Melt Pool Forms 🔥
When metal and energy meet: Metal → Melts → Creates Molten Metal Pool → Solidifies → Forms a Layer.
This small molten region is called: Melt Pool. The melt pool is where the actual manufacturing happens.
Step 5: The Part Begins to Grow
The machine moves according to the CAD design. Material is continuously deposited. Layer after layer. Bead after
bead. Until the final metal component is completed.
🏗️ DED Construction Flow
Metal Wire / Powder → Deposition Head → Laser / Plasma / Electron Beam → Melt Pool → Part Formation.

5. Material Jetting
🚀 Imagine a 3D Printer That Works Like an Inkjet Printer
Have you ever seen an inkjet printer printing on paper? It sprays thousands of tiny droplets of ink to create an
image. Now imagine replacing: 🖋️ Ink with 💧 Liquid Photopolymer Material and printing layer upon layer until a 3D
object is created. That's exactly how Material Jetting works.
🤔 What is Material Jetting?
Material Jetting is a 3D printing technology that creates objects by depositing tiny droplets of liquid photopolymer
material and instantly curing them using UV light. Think of it as: "3D Printing with Tiny Drops of Liquid Plastic"
The printer deposits microscopic droplets exactly where needed and then solidifies them using UV light.
Why is Material Jetting Special?
Most 3D printing technologies focus on:

🎬 What Happens When You Click Print?
Let's follow a tiny droplet through its journey.
Step 1: Meet Mr. Material Drop 💧
Everything begins inside a material reservoir. This reservoir stores liquid photopolymer materials. Think of it as:
An Ink Cartridge for a 3D Printer.
Common Materials
🔵 Rigid Photopolymer
🟢 Transparent Material
🟠 Flexible Material
🟣 Multi-Color Materials
Step 2: The Print Head Starts Working
Now the printer activates the:
Print Head
This component is similar to the print head of an inkjet printer. Its job is to spray thousands of tiny droplets.
Think About It
A single droplet is often smaller than the width of a human hair. Thousands of droplets combine to create each
layer.
Responsibilities
✅ Material Deposition
✅ Layer Formation
✅ Precision Placement
Step 3: Droplet Deposition Begins
The print head moves across the build area. Tiny droplets are deposited exactly where required. Imagine This:
Drawing a picture using thousands of microscopic water droplets. That's how each layer is created.
What Makes It Unique?
Unlike FDM: No filament is melted.
Unlike SLA: No resin tank is required.
The material is deposited directly where needed.
Step 4: UV Light Instantly Cures the Material 🔵
Immediately after deposition: UV lamps shine on the droplets.
What Happens?
Liquid Droplet → UV Light → Solid Material.
The material hardens almost instantly.
Interactive Question
❓ What happens if UV light is not applied?
✅ The droplets remain liquid and the print fails.
Step 5: Layer by Layer Growth
After one layer is cured: The build platform moves. Another layer is deposited. Another layer is cured. The process
repeats until the complete model is formed.
🏗️ Material Jetting Construction Flow
Material Reservoir → Print Head → Droplet Deposition → UV Curing → Part Formation.

6. PolyJet Technology
🚀 Imagine a Color Printer That Can Print Real Objects. Have you ever used a color inkjet printer? It sprays tiny
droplets of ink onto paper to create images. Now imagine a machine that sprays microscopic droplets of liquid
plastic instead of ink and instantly hardens them using UV light. The result? A Real 3D Object with Amazing Detail
and Surface Finish. This technology is called: 🌈 PolyJet Technology. PolyJet is one of the most advanced and
realistic 3D printing technologies available today. It is widely used when appearance, detail, color accuracy, and
realism are extremely important.
🤔 What Makes PolyJet Special?
Most 3D printers can print:
One material
One color
Basic surface finish
PolyJet can print:
✅ Multiple Materials
✅ Multiple Colors
✅ Transparent Parts
✅ Flexible Parts
✅ Rubber-like Materials
✅ Extremely Fine Details
All in a single print.
Fun Fact
Some PolyJet printers can simulate:
Soft rubber
Hard plastic
Transparent glass-like materials
in the same model. This makes prototypes look almost identical to final products.
🎬 What Happens When You Click Print?
Let's follow a tiny droplet through its journey.
Step 1: Meet Mr. Photopolymer 💧
Everything begins inside a material cartridge. The cartridge stores: Liquid Photopolymer Material. Think of it as: A
giant ink cartridge filled with liquid plastic.
Types of Materials
🔵 Rigid Materials
🟢 Flexible Materials
🟡 Transparent Materials
🔴 Colored Materials
Interactive Question
❓ What is a photopolymer?
✅ A liquid material that becomes solid when exposed to UV light.
Step 2: The Jetting Head Starts Spraying
The printer activates the: Jetting Head. This is one of the most important components in a PolyJet printer. Its job
is to deposit thousands of microscopic droplets.
Think About It. Imagine spraying millions of tiny water droplets exactly where needed. That is how each layer is
created.
Responsibilities
✅ Material Deposition
✅ Layer Creation
✅ Multi-Material Printing
Step 3: UV Light Instantly Hardens the Material 🔵
Immediately after the droplets are deposited: UV lamps shine on them.
What Happens?
Liquid Material → UV Light → Solid Layer.
This process happens within seconds.
Real-Life Analogy: Imagine sunlight instantly turning water into solid plastic. That's essentially what happens
inside the printer.
Step 4: Layer Formation Begins
The printer deposits: Droplets → Cures Them → Deposits More Droplets → Cures Again.
This process repeats hundreds or thousands of times.
Eventually: A complete 3D object is formed.
Step 5: Finished Part Appears
The result is often so realistic that people mistake it for a final manufactured product.
🏗️ PolyJet Construction Flow
Photopolymer Cartridge → Jetting Head → UV Lamp → Layer Formation → Finished Part.
